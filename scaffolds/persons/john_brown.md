# Person Scaffold - John Brown

Actor ID: `brown`

Role groups:

- `abolitionist_prophetic`
- `prophetic_moral_voice`

---

## Analytical Purpose

Brown tests the most extreme abolitionist action-orientation in the project:
slavery as sin and captivity, violence as divine obligation, and martyrdom as
redemptive testimony.

---

## Primary Cluster Priors

Tight design:

1. `sin`
2. `blood`
3. `martyrdom`

Expanded priors:

- slavery as sin and blood guilt
- violence as purification or deliverance
- divine law over human law
- martyrdom and sacrifice
- enslaved people as captives to be delivered
- judgment day and apocalypse

## Starter Corpus

Source anchors:

- John Brown address broadside, Library of Congress:
  https://www.loc.gov/item/2021770281/
- Avalon Project, trial and execution materials:
  https://avalon.law.yale.edu/19th_century/john_brown.asp
- John Brown's Speech to the Court, Wikisource:
  https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/John_Brown%27s_Speech_to_the_Court_at_his_Trial

Recommended documents:

1. Provisional Constitution and Ordinances for the People of the United States,
   1858 - political/revolutionary text.
2. Speech to the Court, November 2, 1859 - formal courtroom address.
3. Selected jail letters, 1859 - private/semi-public letters.
4. Final note or last letters before execution, 1859 - martyrdom threshold.
5. Earlier Kansas letters if available - violent action baseline.

---

## Disconfirmation Tests

- If Brown's rhetoric is more constitutional than biblical, revise the
  prophetic prior.
- If enslaved people appear mostly as beneficiaries rather than agents, mark the
  agency gap even in abolitionist rhetoric.
- If violence is framed as rescue rather than punishment, classify carefully
  under `captivity_and_deliverance` rather than `punishment_and_theodicy`.
